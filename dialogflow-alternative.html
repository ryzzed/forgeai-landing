When ChatFlow is the better choice

ChatFlow is purpose-built for one use case: small businesses that want AI chat on their website

You don't need to train intents

Dialogflow requires you to define intents (what the user wants), training phrases (how they might say it), and entities (variables to extract). ChatFlow uses a large language model — you describe your business in plain English and it figures out the rest.

You want conversation, not scripts

Dialogflow's intent-based architecture handles questions it was trained on and falls back on everything else. ChatFlow handles any question that fits within your system prompt — gracefully handling out-of-scope questions without hard breaks in the conversation.

You need lead capture, not fulfillment

Dialogflow's fulfillment webhooks are built for triggering backend actions — booking systems, CRMs, databases. ChatFlow is optimized for the simpler, higher-ROI use case: capturing a name and phone number for a service business callback.

You want predictable monthly pricing

Dialogflow pricing scales with request volume and can become unpredictable at scale. ChatFlow is $39/month for 5,000 conversations — flat fee, no per-request surprises, no enterprise negotiation required.

Common questions

When does Dialogflow make more sense than ChatFlow?

If you're building a chatbot as a feature inside a software product — connecting to your own backend APIs, triggering booking confirmations, looking up order status in a database — Dialogflow's fulfillment architecture is designed for that. If you need a voice assistant or IVR integration, Dialogflow has Google's telephony infrastructure. If you need deep customization of the NLU pipeline itself, or you're training a domain-specific model, Dialogflow gives you that control. ChatFlow is for adding conversational AI to a business website, not building a product feature.

Can ChatFlow handle the same conversational quality as Dialogflow?

For small business use cases, ChatFlow's LLaMA 3.3 70B model produces more natural conversations than Dialogflow's intent-classification architecture — because it's a large language model that understands context, not a pattern matcher that looks for trained phrases. The tradeoff is control: Dialogflow's scripted flows are highly predictable, while LLM-based systems require a well-written system prompt to stay on-topic. For answering questions about your business and capturing leads, the LLM approach is easier to configure and performs better in practice.

How do I migrate from Dialogflow to ChatFlow?

There's no technical migration — you don't export Dialogflow intents and import them to ChatFlow. Instead, look at your Dialogflow intents as a list of what your chatbot should know: take the information behind each intent and turn it into a paragraph in your ChatFlow system prompt. Most small business Dialogflow implementations can be replicated with a 300-500 word system prompt. The result is usually more flexible and handles edge cases that fell outside the trained intents.
