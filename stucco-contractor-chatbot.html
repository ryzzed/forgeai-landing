Why stucco contractors use ChatFlow

Crack urgency triage

Stucco cracks range from cosmetic to structurally significant — configure ChatFlow to ask customers to describe what they see and triage accordingly. Hairline cracks: low urgency, patch and blend. Map cracking: recoat needed but not emergency. Wide cracks or staining below windows: moisture infiltration risk, flag for priority scheduling. Customers who understand their crack type isn't just cosmetic are more motivated to schedule quickly — and moisture-related damage customers close at a much higher rate because they understand the risk of delay.

EIFS vs hard-coat identification

EIFS (synthetic stucco, Dryvit) and traditional hard-coat stucco look similar but require completely different repair approaches — and EIFS has a much higher risk of concealed moisture damage. Configure ChatFlow to ask about home age (post-1985 EIFS era) and stucco feel (slightly soft/spongy = EIFS vs hard as concrete = traditional). EIFS inquiries are routed with a note that probe moisture testing may be needed before scoping the repair. Pre-identifying EIFS prevents estimators from arriving with the wrong repair approach.

Full recoat vs patch decision

Stucco patch jobs that don't match the existing texture and color are a common customer complaint — configure ChatFlow to explain the recoat-vs-patch tradeoff honestly. When map cracking is widespread or the existing finish has significant fading, a full recoat of the affected elevation is often the right recommendation rather than visible patches. Customers who understand why a full recoat is sometimes better value than patching are less likely to resist the recommendation at estimate time — and they close faster.

Painting and color change bundling

Many stucco recoat customers also want to change the home's color — configure ChatFlow to ask whether the customer is happy with the current color or wants to update it. A recoat over existing stucco in a new color is often no additional cost (same application process). Elastomeric paint applied over stucco ($1,500–3,500 for a typical home) is sometimes an alternative to recoat for customers on a tighter budget — surfacing this option positions you as the advisor rather than the salesperson.

How does the chatbot explain the difference between hairline cracks and structural stucco damage?

Hairline cracks under 1/16 inch: cosmetic, low urgency. Map/alligator cracking: base coat failure or moisture — recoat needed. Step cracks following mortar joints: possible foundation movement. Wide cracks or staining: moisture infiltration — urgent repair. The chatbot routes moisture-related damage for priority scheduling and explains why delay increases repair scope.

Traditional hard coat (hard as concrete) is priced and scoped directly from the description.
