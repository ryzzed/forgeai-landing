How to Write a Chatbot System Prompt for Your Business (With Templates)

Your AI chatbot is only as good as the instructions you give it. A well-written system prompt turns a generic AI into a knowledgeable assistant that represents your business accurately. A poor one produces vague, off-brand, or useless responses. This guide shows you exactly what to include.

What is a chatbot system prompt?

A system prompt is the set of instructions you give your AI chatbot before it talks to any customers. It tells the AI who it is, what your business does, what questions it should answer, how it should respond, and what it should never do.

Think of it as an employee briefing. If you were training a new front desk person, you'd tell them: "We're a dental office, we accept these insurance plans, our hours are X, here's how to book an appointment, and never give medical advice." Your system prompt is the same thing — just for AI.

Key insight: The AI doesn't "know" anything about your business unless you tell it. A specific, detailed system prompt produces dramatically better responses than a vague one. Time spent on your system prompt is time spent improving every future conversation.

The 6-part system prompt structure

Every effective business chatbot system prompt should cover these six areas:

1. Identity and role

Start by telling the AI who it is and what it's there to do. This sets the tone for everything that follows.

Example: "You are a helpful assistant for Maple Street Dental, a family dental practice in Austin, Texas. Your job is to answer patient questions about our services, accepted insurance, and how to schedule appointments."

2. Business facts

Include everything a knowledgeable staff member would know: location, hours, services, pricing (even rough ranges), accepted payment/insurance, parking, contact information, and website links for more detail.

3. Tone and personality

Tell the AI how it should communicate. Friendly and casual? Professional and formal? Brief and direct? Warm and reassuring? This should match your brand.

Example: "Be warm and approachable. Use simple, friendly language — avoid medical jargon. Responses should be concise (2-4 sentences per answer) unless a detailed explanation is genuinely helpful."

4. What to help with

Explicitly list the topics and tasks the chatbot should handle. This focuses the AI and prevents it from wandering off-topic.

5. Guardrails — what NOT to do

This is critical. Tell the AI what it must never do. This prevents hallucinations, off-topic responses, and anything that could create liability.

6. Lead capture instructions

Tell the AI when and how to ask for contact information. This is where your chatbot converts conversations into leads.

What to include (and what to leave out)

Always include:

  • Business name, address, phone number, and email
  • Business hours (including holidays if relevant)
  • Services or products offered with brief descriptions
  • Pricing ranges or how pricing works (even "starting at X")
  • Geographic service area or delivery zone
  • How to book, schedule, or get a quote
  • Your most-asked questions and their answers
  • What happens after someone contacts you (response time, next steps)

Leave out or handle carefully:

  • Guarantees or promises you can't honor in every case
  • Exact prices for custom work — use ranges instead
  • Competitor names (unless you want to make comparisons, which usually backfires)
  • Internal information — supplier names, margins, internal processes
  • Employee names and personal info
  • Anything time-sensitive that will become outdated (specific promotions, temporary closures)

Update your prompt when things change. Changed your hours? Added a new service? Updated pricing? Revise your system prompt. Stale information in a system prompt is worse than no information — the AI will confidently give customers wrong answers.

Setting topic guardrails

Guardrails tell the AI what it's not allowed to discuss. These are essential for staying on-brand, avoiding liability, and keeping conversations focused on what matters.

The basic guardrail pattern:

"Only answer questions related to [your business domain]. If a visitor asks about anything unrelated to [domain], politely redirect them: 'I'm here to help with [business type] questions — for anything else, feel free to [email/call us]'."

Industry-specific guardrails to consider:

  • Healthcare: "Never provide diagnoses, medical advice, or treatment recommendations. Always direct medical questions to our clinical staff or advise calling 911 for emergencies."
  • Legal: "Do not provide legal advice or opinions on specific legal matters. Direct case-specific questions to schedule a consultation."
  • Financial services: "Do not provide investment advice or specific financial recommendations. All investment decisions require a consultation with a licensed advisor."
  • General small business: "Do not make commitments about pricing, timelines, or availability without noting that these require confirmation from our team."

Restaurant chatbot template

System prompt
You are a helpful assistant for [Restaurant Name], a [cuisine type] restaurant located at [address] in [city]. Business hours: [days and times] Reservations: [how to book — phone number or booking link] Party sizes: Up to [X] guests for standard reservations. Private events for larger groups available with advance notice. Menu overview: We serve [brief description — e.g., "authentic Italian pasta, wood-fired pizza, and seasonal specials"]. Our full menu is at [URL]. Popular questions: - Parking: [parking situation] - Dietary options: [vegetarian/vegan/gluten-free availability] - Private dining: [yes/no, contact info] - Gift cards: [yes/no, how to purchase] Your job: Answer questions about our menu, hours, reservations, and policies. If someone wants to make a reservation, direct them to [phone/link]. For group bookings over [X] people, collect their name, contact info, date, and party size so our events team can follow up. Do not: Make promises about wait times or specific table availability. Do not discuss competitor restaurants. Keep responses brief and warm.

Testing and improving your prompt

A first draft is just a starting point. The best system prompts are refined over time based on how visitors actually interact with your chatbot.

How to test your system prompt:

  1. Ask every question your customers regularly ask. Go through your last 20 phone calls or emails — what did people want to know? Try those questions in the chatbot and review the answers critically.
  2. Try off-topic questions. Ask the chatbot something completely unrelated to your business. Does it handle it gracefully and redirect? If it goes off the rails, tighten your guardrails.
  3. Ask edge cases. Try questions at the boundary of what you've configured — "Can you guarantee same-day service?" "What if I'm not satisfied?" The AI's answer will reveal gaps in your prompt.
  4. Test your lead capture flow. Have the chatbot answer a few questions and then see if it naturally asks for contact info. If it doesn't, add explicit lead capture instructions to your prompt.

Common problems and fixes:

  • Answers are too vague → Add more specific facts to your prompt. The AI can only be specific about what you've told it.
  • Chatbot goes off-topic → Strengthen your guardrail language. Add specific examples of what NOT to discuss.
  • Responses are too long → Add: "Keep responses to 2-4 sentences unless a detailed explanation is clearly warranted."
  • Chatbot never asks for contact info → Add explicit instructions: "After answering 1-2 questions, ask if they'd like to [schedule / get a quote / be contacted]."
  • Wrong tone → Add more specific tone instructions. Instead of "be friendly," try "use a warm, conversational tone — like a knowledgeable friend, not a formal business."
