We have a persistent musty smell in the first floor of the house — seems worse in winter. Also noticed our floors feel a little springy near the kitchen. House was built in 1962 and I don't think the crawl space has ever been addressed.
Those are both classic crawl space moisture symptoms. The musty smell is almost always mold or mildew growing on the floor joists — warm, humid air rises into the living space and you smell it most in winter when the house is closed up. The springy floor can mean the wood has been absorbing moisture for years and is starting to soften.

A 1962 home with an unaddressed crawl space typically has just a bare dirt floor — no vapor barrier at all. Moisture evaporates from the soil directly into your living space.

The fix is encapsulation: a heavy-duty liner (20-mil) sealed to the walls and floor, vents sealed, and usually a dehumidifier to control residual humidity. For a typical crawl space under a 1,500 sqft footprint:

• Full encapsulation: $4,500–9,000
• Plus dehumidifier: add $800–1,500

If there's active mold on the joists, that's addressed first or alongside — we'll assess that at the inspection.

Scope pre-qualification for inspectors

Configure ChatFlow to ask about crawl space square footage, current moisture barrier status (bare dirt, old plastic, or existing liner), and whether mold or damaged wood has been seen. Inspectors who arrive knowing the home was built in 1962 with a bare dirt floor and visible mold have a completely different inspection than one arriving cold. Pre-qualified scopes reduce inspection time, increase close rate at the inspection, and prevent the "I didn't know that would be extra" customer friction.

Dehumidifier and drainage add-on capture

Full encapsulation is rarely complete without a dehumidifier — and homes with standing water or high water tables need a sump pump. Configure ChatFlow to ask whether the customer has noticed water pooling after heavy rain, and whether the home is in a low-lying area. A dehumidifier ($800–1,500) is a near-universal add-on; a sump pump ($1,200–2,500) surfaces the subset of customers who genuinely need one. Capturing these during the chat means the inspector can confirm and quote them at the visit rather than introducing them as surprises.

Mold remediation routing

Active mold on floor joists or subfloor requires remediation before or alongside encapsulation — a scope addition that customers don't always expect. Configure ChatFlow to ask whether visible mold has been noticed and route these inquiries with a note for the inspector. Customers who learn upfront that mold remediation may be needed alongside the encapsulation are more prepared for a larger proposal and less likely to delay or decline because of an unexpected cost item at estimate time.
