Basic diagnostic intake

Appliance type, brand, model if available, age, and what symptoms it's showing. This context helps you arrive with likely parts and tools — reducing the "need to order parts" callbacks that frustrate customers and extend your job time.

Service fee transparency

Your diagnostic/service call fee, what it covers, and whether it applies toward the repair. This is the question every appliance repair customer has — answering it in chat before they ask it on the phone saves time and filters out price shoppers who won't book.

Repair vs replace guidance

Give the honest framework — appliance age, repair cost relative to replacement — so homeowners know you're looking out for them. Customers who feel advised rather than sold to return for future repairs and refer neighbors.

Service area and scheduling

Zip code confirmation, typical availability, and whether same-day service is possible. Homeowners with a broken appliance want to know when you can come — the bot sets that expectation immediately rather than making them wait for a callback to find out.

Common questions

What appliances should I list as outside my scope?

Be explicit: "We repair washers, dryers, refrigerators, dishwashers, ovens/ranges, and microwaves. We don't currently service small appliances (blenders, coffee makers, toasters), window AC units, or built-in appliances like wine coolers." Scope clarity prevents you from showing up to a job you can't complete — and helps the homeowner quickly identify whether they've found the right technician before investing in a service call appointment.

How do I handle warranty questions?

"If your appliance is under manufacturer warranty, the repair may be covered — contact the manufacturer first. If it's expired or you're past the extended warranty period, we handle repairs independently. We don't have any manufacturer warranty contracts, but our repairs come with a 90-day warranty on parts and labor." This protects you from doing a warranty repair you won't get reimbursed for, and gives customers clarity on their options.

Should the chatbot give specific cost estimates before the technician diagnoses?

Give ranges for common repairs, not exact quotes: "Refrigerator not cooling: common causes are a faulty compressor ($300-500 repair), dirty condenser coils (free DIY or $85 service call), or a failed defrost system ($150-250). We can't confirm the cause until we see it." Ranges with common causes are helpful to homeowners deciding whether to call vs replace. Specific quotes before diagnosis set expectations you may not be able to meet — and can lose you the job if you're wrong in the direction the customer doesn't like.
