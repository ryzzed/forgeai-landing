The questions parents and adult learners ask before signing up

Instrument and teacher matching

Guitar, piano, violin, voice, drums — describe each instrument's suitability by age and learning style. Match prospective students to the right teacher based on age, goals, and musical interest.

Pricing and lesson structure

30 vs. 45 vs. 60-minute lessons, monthly packages vs. pay-per-lesson, group vs. private — explain your pricing structure clearly so families know what to expect before their first call.

Age and starting level FAQ

Minimum age for each instrument, beginner vs. intermediate programs, adult learner tracks — answer "is my child ready?" and "I haven't played in 20 years, can I start?" without interrupting a lesson.

In-person vs. online lessons

If you offer online lessons, explain how they work, what's required (instrument, device, internet), and how the experience compares to in-person. Capture students outside your geographic area.

Common questions

Can I use this as a solo instructor, not a full school?

Absolutely — many of our music teacher customers are independent instructors teaching out of their home studio or a rented space. The chatbot works the same way: add your instrument, schedule availability, pricing, and teaching philosophy to the system prompt. Parents and adult students get answers at 9pm when they're browsing and you're done teaching for the day.

What about recital and performance questions?

Include your recital schedule, format (formal vs. informal), whether recitals are required, and any associated costs in the system prompt. Parents researching music schools often care deeply about recitals — having clear, immediate answers helps them choose you over a school where this information is hard to find.

Can it handle questions in different languages?

ChatFlow's underlying LLM (LLaMA 3.3 70B) supports dozens of languages. If you serve a multilingual community, the bot can respond in the language the visitor writes in without any extra configuration. Many music schools in urban areas serve families where English is a second language — this is a meaningful advantage for capturing those enrollments.
